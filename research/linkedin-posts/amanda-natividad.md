Do you know about ghost citations?

Kevin Indig describes this as when AI cites you/your brand as a source
in its answer without actually naming you.

So the AI retrieves your content, uses it to form its answer, maybe even
cites your page... but doesn't say it's you.

Kevin gave a great example on this week's Zero Click Marketing
podcast:

Ask an AI tool "what's the best CRM?" and it may pull heavily from G2
to form the answer. But the answer itself is more likely to say HubSpot,
Salesforce, etc. than "according to G2."

G2 gets used. The CRM brands get named. The user gets their query
answered. This particular use case might be fine for G2 (it is, after
all, their business model to be the credible source, not the beloved
brand).

But overall, what this means for marketers is that when you're
measuring AI visibility, citations are only part of the picture.

You also need to ask:

• Are we being mentioned by name?
• Are we showing up in the shortlist?
• Are we associated with the right attributes --- trusted, reliable,
effective, affordable, premium, whatever actually matters in our
category?

Ghost citations are the AI-search version of zero-click influence:

Your content may be shaping demand even though your brand stays
invisible.

Kevin and I dig into this
on this week's Zero Click Marketing podcast.

AI visibility is your newest leading indicator of demand.

If buyers are asking AI tools for recommendations, comparisons, and
shortlists (and yes, they are), then your presence in those answers
tells you whether you're even up for consideration before someone ever
visits your website.

Similarweb's "Downstream Impact of AI Visibility" study found that when
brands were recommended in AI answers, users were more likely to visit
those brands' sites in the following 7 days.

Naturally, that impact didn't normally show up as a clean AI referral
click. It showed up later as branded search or direct traffic.

That's demand. Messy and hard to attribute, but it's demand. This is
where AI visibility can help you figure out what to do next. You can
then start thinking about:

- Are we showing up?
- Are we being described accurately?
- Are we being compared to the right alternatives?
- Are our strongest proof points making it into the public record?
- Are buyers seeing us in the moments where preferences are being
formed?

If you're absent from those answers most or all of the time, then this
is about more than clicks. You may be missing from the moment when
buyers form the mental shortlist.

And if you are showing up, the next question is whether you're being
framed accurately enough to earn the follow-up branded search, the
direct visit, or the shortlist spot.

## Post 4 — 1 Week Ago

Attachment: None

The inflection point that started my "zero click marketing" journey:
It was 2014 and I was doing B2B marketing at Fitbit...

We were in the business of employee benefits and corporate wellness. The
obvious SEO play was:

- Find the high-volume keywords.
- Create content for them.
- Rank 1st for "corporate wellness," etc.
- Step 4: Profit (I guess?)

But even back then that felt wrong to me.

Because the people searching "what is corporate wellness?" probably
weren't the people buying corporate wellness programs.

The buyers already knew the category. They already had internal
politics, budget constraints, implementation questions, vendor fatigue,
and a million reasons to be skeptical.

So the impactful work wasn't in ranking for the most common beginner
keyword. It was to understand the audience well enough to make things
that actually met them where they were.

That's the part I didn't have language for yet.

But looking back, it was audience research.

And that's still what informs Zero Click Marketing today.

If you're marketing to an informed audience, you can't just chase
low-hanging fruit topics. You have to get on their level. You have to
speak their language. You have to understand what they already know,
what they're tired of hearing, and what they're still trying to
solve.

That work is harder.

It's also the work that earns credibility.

Zero Click Marketing isn't simply to create content for platforms where
people already spend time.

It's making things so useful, specific, and resonant that people
remember who helped them before they were ready to buy.

And doing that starts with knowing the audience.
