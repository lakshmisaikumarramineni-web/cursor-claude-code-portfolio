Attachment: PDF

9 Hours Ago

 Your brand can rank in one AI engine and vanish in another.

PDF carousel: "The Ghost Citation Problem" (9 slides)

Slide summary:
1. Title: The Ghost Citation Problem — AI cites your content, but not your brand
2. The analysis: based on Semrush AI Visibility Toolkit data — 3,981 domain queries, 115 prompts, 14 countries, 4 LLMs across ChatGPT, Gemini, AI Overviews, and AI Mode. Finding: a source link rarely guarantees brand recognition.
3. The citation gap: most brands assume being cited means being seen — the data says otherwise; brand citations are often functionally invisible to the end user.
4. Each LLM behaves differently: Gemini cites less but mentions more brands by name; ChatGPT does the opposite.
5. Strong brands get named in the text more than weak ones — LLMs transfer information more readily than brand recognition, using publisher sites as sources but rarely naming them.
6. LLMs disagree with each other: across 454 prompt+domain combinations tested, 22% of outputs showed disagreement on whether to mention the brand at all.
7. Brand mention rates vary by geography, even controlling for the LLM used (not a language confound — prompts were localized).
8. Teaser for the full memo: covers why short prompts show brands more, why comparative prompts drive more mentions, which content formats convert citations into mentions, and how to audit prompts where your brand disappears.

## Post 3 — 1 Day Ago
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_a-benchmark-only-earns-ai-citations-where-activity-7480191394486243328-_TVs
Attachment: None

A benchmark only earns AI citations where the question has a measurable
comparison behind it.
I sorted Gauge's citation set by vertical. Primary-research citations
clustered on prompts that asked AI to rank options on specs: speed,
cost, latency, yield, performance. Cloud data warehouses, crypto
staking, MEV. Anywhere a buyer wants "which is best" backed by
numbers.
The pattern vanished everywhere else. B2B SaaS and CRM, TEFL education,
product analytics returned listicles, product pages, and explainers.
After cleaning the set, not one of those topics produced a cited
primary-research page.
Before you commission a data study, check that your topic has a
comparison worth measuring. A benchmark with no "versus" behind it
gives AI nothing to lift.
The opening is the vertical where buyers ask "which is best" and
nobody has published a real dataset to answer it.

citations. The format that actually gets cited is narrower than
"original data wins."

yardstick, speed, cost, latency, and publishes the results as numbers.

Benchmark are assets because they deliver a number only your product can
produce, packaged as a comparison a buyer can act on.

Everyone says publish original research. But who tests whether AI cites
the brand that produced the number or the page that surfaces it most
cleanly?

The answer is uncomfortable: an aggregator can repackage your benchmark
into an answer-ready page and collect the citation your research
earned.

Owning the data gets you into the fight. It doesn't win it.

I found DATE and NUMBER are the entity types that predict ChatGPT
citations most. A precise figure surfaced high on the page beats the
same figure buried in narrative, even when the buried one came first.
The aggregator wins on presentation and on the trust AI already assigns
its domain.

The work doesn't stop at publishing. Structure the number for
extraction, and build the off-site authority that puts your domain in
AI's trusted set for the topic.

## Post 8 — 1 Week Ago
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_shorter-focused-content-wins-in-chatgpt-activity-7478009571274514439-JjI2
Attachment: PDF

More content does not mean more citations in ChatGPT.

PDF carousel: "ChatGPT Rewards Focused Pages" (9 slides)

Slide summary:
1. Title: ChatGPT Rewards Focused Pages — the "ultimate guide" loses to a focused page that covers 2-3 related angles well.
2. The study: with AirOps, ran 16,851 queries through ChatGPT 3 times each, capturing every fanout query, URL, citation, and page — 353,799 pages, 815,484 query-page pairs, ~2 fanout queries per prompt, ~10 URLs retrieved per sub-search on average.
3. Density barely moves the needle: among pages with strong query match, moderate coverage outperforms exhaustive coverage.
4. What predicts citation — two dominant signals: (1) retrieval rank is the gatekeeper (position 0 = 58% citation rate vs. position 10 = 14%); (2) heading match is the strongest content signal (0.90+ match = 41% citation rate vs. below 0.50 = 30%).
5. The Wikipedia exception: worst retrieval rank and lowest query match, yet highest citation rate overall — wins because it's exhaustive, structured, and cross-linked at a scale publishers can't replicate.
6. The bimodal reality: always-cited and never-cited pages average nearly identical stats (~2,200 words, ~20 headings, ~54 domain authority) — retrieval rank, not content quality alone, separates winners from losers.
7. What this means for your content: match the query directly in the heading; aim for 500-2,000 words; use 7-20 subheadings (more isn't better); audit your longest pages first; prioritize price/cost/how-to content.
8. Teaser for the full memo: density-vs-query-match tradeoff by vertical, patterns from 6.8M analyzed H2-H4 headings, "best x" search insights, and 6 test recommendations.
9. CTA slide: "Refocus your content library" — read the full memo in Growth Memo Premium.
