You're underestimating personalization in AI Search.

Yesterday I joined Crystal
Carter and
Ashley Segura for a
Wix webinar on personalization and AI Search.

Spoiler: They're smart, and we had fun. I'm sure the replay will be
accessible soon because it's 2026 and that's how we do.

But I wanted to clarify how I frame AI Search personalization for my own
mental model.

AI Search feels a lot like local search, but with even more context.

When someone searches "pizza near me," "doctor near me," or "vacation
spot near me," proximity dictates the answer.

A pizza place across the country is technically a pizza place.

It's still a bad result for me when I'm hungry at 10:00 pm on a Saturday
night. Alright, it's been a few years since I've ordered a late-night
pizza, but 20-something Garrett remembers.

Now apply that same logic beyond location.

AI Search can factor in:

• past searches
• past context (everything you'd asked or shared historically - which is
a lot if you're treating AI like a therapist)
• emails
• photos
• saved preferences
• saved content
• location
• inferred intent

Two people can ask the same question and get very different
recommendations. We know this. Ain't a bug, it's a feature. It's a
better experience. That's what it should be.

Sucks for marketers. Strike that. Sucks for bad marketers that haven't
invested in audience and market research.

But it *should* change strategy. Brands won't need to rank for a
query. That's a different mental model.

We need to be the right recommendation for the right context. You know,
fundamental marketing.

And think about how we're searching. I've been using dictation. I
provide SO MUCH MORE CONTEXT. And I bet a lot of your audience are
moving in that direction too.

That's why we need to fan out our content, like AI fans out searches. AI
Search can't read your mind and assume your personas and your product
features.

You need to connect the dots, write the narrative, get the message out
on your own properties and everyone else's. How you do it is half the
battle. GI JOE.

Here's my SEO Week presentation on personalization, shared publicly for
the first time. (With a fun and accurate intro by Michael
King)

Through the lens of Google Personal Intelligence and the experiment I
ran with my team, I argued AI Search is making context unavoidable.

We know this. But we can't gloss over it when we're using lab tools to
build strategy.

Here's how I framed it:

There's the context we give search engines on purpose.

I can tell Google, ChatGPT, or Gemini that I'm traveling with my family,
that I have a certain budget, that I care about safety, speed, price,
taste, or convenience. That explicit context changes the answer.

Then there's the context we don't always think about.

In the presentation, I showed examples from Google AI Mode where Gmail,
Google Photos, receipts, newsletters, direct mail scans, and personal
signals started influencing recommendations. One result pulled from a
USPS Daily Digest email. Another picked up brands from emails I had
seeded into an account. In one case, AI Mode referenced my family when
recommending streaming services.

Every conversation with AI Search branches out both ways like some
deformed Möbius strip or a game of Calvinball.

Context in. Context layered on. Context out. Rinse and repeat.

This gets even messier once you factor in Google Personal Intelligence,
ChatGPT Memory, and the personalization layers being built across AI
search platforms.

A prompt is no longer just a prompt.

A search is no longer just a search.

It's the query plus explicit context, plus inferred context, plus
implicit context, plus platform memory.

Love or hate personas and market research, we need to understand how
different audiences describe themselves, what signals surround them,
what shows up in their inboxes, what brands they already interact with,
and how those signals may influence what AI systems recommend.

That's why we looked at personas, seeded accounts, personal
intelligence, citations, brand lift, and the weird ways personalization
can reshape discovery.

Personalization is messy. It is uneven. It is sometimes useful. It is
sometimes creepy. And it is going to make AI Search measurement much
harder than traditional rank tracking.

Directional data doesn't feel like a good enough answer for the C-Suite.
Education and storytelling can't be the amuse bouche. It's gotta be your
freakin' entrée.

me up. But honestly, you had to be in the room.

## Post 5 — 2 Weeks Ago
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/garrettsussman_you-cant-willy-nilly-your-ai-search-program-activity-7475548847578959872-HRIU
Attachment: Images

You can't willy-nilly your AI Search program.

Organic AI Search is too big, the models change too often, and the
visibility signals come from too many places.

Brands need a department responsible for connecting the work across SEO,
content, digital PR, social, reviews, video, partnerships, and product
marketing.

AI Search can't sit with one person running random prompt checks every
few weeks. It can't only live in the SEO team, I'm sorry.

I got early access to new research from Samanyou
Garg and the
Writesonic team.
It's a complex channel that everyone needs to experiment with:

• Reddit citations jumped 4.4x after a single ChatGPT model update.
• 72% of sources showed up on only one AI Search engine.
• 90% of the time a brand was mentioned in an answer, another brand got
dropped.

We don't fully understand the impact of different models and model
changes, but it completely disrupts any semblance of static, reliable
visibility.

A source that works in ChatGPT may never show up in Gemini, Perplexity,
or AI Overviews.

And AI answers don't create infinite room for every brand, nor would we
want it to. Most of the time, one brand getting included means another
brand loses visibility. It's still zero-sum.

So the question can't just be, "Do we show up?"

The market and audience research questions need to be:

• Which platforms matter most for our buyers?
• Which sources influence those engines?
• Which competitors show up now, and what's sourcing them?
• What evidence would make the model choose us instead?
• How can we best monitor all types of sentiment and improve our
reputation?

An AI Search department needs to monitor citations, brand mentions,
source volatility, answer changes, and competitor displacement over
time. It can be a task force until you get your integrated marketing
team established. But mature companies can't wait for siloed marketing
departments to piecemeal the strategy.

AI Search strategy needs a coordinated program.

What's the operational AI Search Ying Yang to Amanda
Natividad's Zero Click
content? Myriam
Jessier's Zero Context
content.

It's beautiful, despite the excellent Shrek Orge Onion analogy in her
stellar post on SEL. 🧅 - (FYI - the Shrek 5 trailer dropped this week)

Most content is written like we have the whole page, but LLMs often pull
one passage at a time. (And let's be honest, too many of us only read
the headline anyway, so maybe we have more in common with AI Search Bots
than we think.)

You don't have to choose between writing for people and writing for
machines.
You need to layer the content so both can use it.

At iPullRank, we've talked about this for a long time: the best content
is useful to humans and legible to machines. The tradeoff is usually
self-inflicted.

The pressure now is at the sentence and passage level.

Myriam asks you to look at your site: Taken out of context, do your
sentences still make sense?

A lot of content doesn't have that lens in its editorial review:

• vague claims
• unclear "it," "this," and "that" references
• copy that delays the point
• sections that need too much surrounding context
• brand mentions without enough positioning around them

We can infer. Retrieval systems need obvious connection.

Dan Petrovic and Kevin
Indig have both looked at
where AI systems source material from across pages. The answer isn't
stable (imo). Sometimes it's the beginning. Sometimes it's the middle.
Sometimes it's somewhere else.

I saw the same pattern in my own Google Personal Intelligence
experiment. Google referenced the middle of a Marco
Giordano newsletter
since that passage was relevant enough on its own.

Too many of us still optimize pages, but AI systems select passages.

Amanda popularized zero-click marketing: create value before the click.

Myriam adds AI Search framing:

Write for zero context in a zero-click ecosystem.

That means content teams need to build sections that are:

• clear after extraction
• specific enough to cite
• useful to the reader
• structured for retrieval
• contextual enough to shape brand perception

Ogres have layers. Just like your neighborhood AEO content strategy.

You make sure the machine gets the bite-sized, clear answer. The human
gets the proof, specificity, and reason to care.

I'll be thinking about this post as I chop onions for tacos tonight.
Crying because AEO is a fickle beast. And because I'm chopping onions.

👆 Take that sentence out of context, baby!
